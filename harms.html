01 — The Charges

Egregious Offenses

Offense 01 — Reproductive Surveillance

A Texas Sheriff Used Flock To Track Women Who Visited Abortion Clinics

"had an abortion, search for female"
— actual logged query justification

A Texas sheriff queried the Flock database to identify vehicles that traveled from out of state to an abortion clinic. The search justification was logged as a single phrase. This is a documented use of the system as deployed. Every municipality running Flock cameras contributed plate reads that made this query possible.

Source: Documented Flock system query log

Offense 02 — Federal Immigration Pipeline

Flock Data Flows Directly To ICE

Through partnerships and subpoena access, Flock Safety data feeds directly into federal immigration enforcement. Local plate reads become ICE intelligence upon request. Your city council voted to buy a "crime-fighting tool." What they purchased was a node in a federal deportation surveillance network.

Source: Flock Safety federal partnership documentation, ICE data access records

Offense 03 — No Consent, No Exit

Every Driver Is Surveilled Without Opt-Out

If you drive past a Flock camera, your plate is read, timestamped, and stored. There is no consent mechanism. There is no opt-out. There is no correction channel. You cannot view your records, challenge their accuracy, or request deletion.

Source: Flock Safety terms of service, municipal contract analysis

Offense 04 — Cross-Jurisdictional Exposure

221 Wisconsin Agencies Query A Single Database

A plate read captured by a single camera in Mauston, Wisconsin is instantly queryable by all 221 agencies in the state's Flock network. Through inter-network sharing, that same read may be accessible to agencies nationwide.

Source: Flock Safety Wisconsin deployment data

Offense 05 — Documented Misuse By Law Enforcement

Wisconsin Police Chief Criminally Charged For Abusing Surveillance Systems

A Wisconsin police chief was criminally charged for using similar automated surveillance infrastructure for personal purposes. The system Flock provides has the same access model, the same query structure, and the same single-word justification logging. The safeguards do not exist because the architecture does not require them.

Source: Wisconsin criminal charging documents

Offense 06 — Private Profit From Public Movement

Your Movement Patterns Are A Corporate Asset

Flock Safety is a private corporation headquartered in Atlanta, Georgia. The movement data collected from municipal cameras — where you go, when you go, how often, who you're near — sits on their corporate balance sheet as a commercial asset. Your city sold your residents' movements to a private company and called it public safety.

Source: Flock Safety corporate filings, municipal contract terms

Offense 07 — The Database Has No Integrity Checks

No Validation, No Correction, No Accountability

Flock's data pipeline performs no validation against actual vehicle registries. Plate reads enter the database on OCR confidence alone. No deduplication audit, no correction mechanism for false reads, no feedback loop for systematic errors. The system that can put you at a location you never visited has no process for you to challenge that record. This is the database law enforcement queries and prosecutors cite — with no integrity guarantees.

Source: FlockBlocker vulnerability research, Flock system architecture analysis

Offense 08 — 1.6 Million Unauthorized Federal Searches

Flock Shared California License Plate Data With Federal Agencies 1.6 Million Times

"vendor-based issue" enabled a "nationwide query" feature
— Oxnard Police Department, upon suspending all Flock cameras

A class action lawsuit filed February 26, 2026 in San Francisco Superior Court revealed that San Francisco Police Department cameras were searched by out-of-state agencies over 1.6 million times in seven months. Los Altos's readers shared data to out-of-state agencies more than a million times. Ventura County found 364,000 unauthorized searches. Oxnard Police suspended all Flock cameras after discovering that despite setting access to "California only," Flock's system allowed nationwide queries. California law explicitly bans sharing ALPR data outside the state. Flock did it 1.6 million times.

Source: Gibbs Mura v. Flock Safety, SF Superior Court (Feb 2026); SFist; Courthouse News Service; CBS Los Angeles

Offense 09 — No Password Required

67 Cameras Streamed Live To The Open Internet With Zero Authentication

Security researchers discovered at least 67 Flock Safety cameras streaming live video to the public internet — no username, no password, no encryption. Anyone could watch real-time footage of playgrounds, parking lots, and public streets. Anyone could view a month of archived footage. Anyone could delete recordings. A 404 Media journalist confirmed by visiting a California traffic signal and watching themselves on camera in real time. Flock called it a "limited misconfiguration." Senator Wyden called for an FTC investigation.

Source: 404 Media, SFist, Privacy Guides (Dec 2025 – Jan 2026); Sen. Wyden FTC referral

Offense 10 — Stolen Credentials On Russian Forums

Flock Login Credentials Are Being Sold On Cybercrime Markets

Cybersecurity firm Hudson Rock found that at least 35 Flock customer accounts had passwords stolen by hackers. Flock login credentials appeared on Russian cybercrime forums. The surveillance system that tracks millions of Americans' movements is secured so poorly that access to it is being sold as a commodity on the dark web. This is the company cities trust with their residents' location data.

Source: Hudson Rock cybersecurity research; Malwarebytes (Feb 2026)

Offense 11 — Surveillance Of Protesters And Activists

Police Used Flock To Track People Exercising Their First Amendment Rights

An Electronic Frontier Foundation investigation in November 2025 documented law enforcement using Flock's ALPR network to surveil protesters exercising their First Amendment rights, target Romani people with discriminatory searches, and track women seeking reproductive healthcare. Every plate read from every municipality on the network made these searches possible.

Source: EFF, "How Cops Are Using Flock Safety's ALPR Network to Surveil Protesters and Activists" (Nov 2025); EFF 2025 Year in Review

Offense 12 — Cameras That Won't Turn Off

Flock Cameras Remained Active After Cities Ordered Them Deactivated

In at least two cities, Flock cameras continued collecting data after officials explicitly ordered them turned off. Eugene, Oregon terminated their contract after a camera was reactivated without authorization. The off switch is a suggestion.

Source: Ban The Cams (Dec 2025); Eugene, OR contract termination records

Offense 13 — Cambridge Secret Installation

Flock Installed Cameras In Cambridge Without Authorization

Cambridge, Massachusetts canceled their Flock contract after discovering that Flock secretly installed two cameras without city authorization. A surveillance company installed surveillance equipment without telling the government that hired them.

Source: Cambridge, MA contract cancellation records (Late 2025)
