01 — The Trick

Grant-Funded Deployment

  • Flock Safety partnered with Lexipol to run a "License Plate Readers Grant Assistance Program." Flock and Lexipol fill out the federal grant paperwork for police departments.
  • Primary federal funding vehicles: DHS Homeland Security Grant Program (SHSP, UASI), DOJ Byrne JAG, and Community Project Funding earmarks.
  • The grant covers year one: hardware, installation, maintenance. The department pays nothing.
  • No city council budget vote required. Grant money bypasses normal procurement and appropriation processes.
  • No public hearing required in most jurisdictions. Cameras go up. Residents find out later, if at all.
  • Santa Cruz, CA: Police explicitly sought SHSP grants for a citywide Flock network covering all city entrances and exits.

The vendor writes the grant application for the product it sells. The department that receives the grant has no incentive to question the vendor's claims. The public that funds the grant has no mechanism to object before cameras are installed.

02 — The Trap

Grant Ends. Subscription Doesn't.

  • Sumner, WA: $50,000 federal grant covered year one entirely. Year two onward: ~$39,000/year, in perpetuity.
  • Once prosecutors build cases on ALPR data and patrol officers rely on alerts, cancellation becomes politically toxic. No chief will be the one who "turned off the crime-fighting tool."
  • Fall River, MA: City declined to fund ShotSpotter's $90,000 annual cost. The company continued providing the service free — maintaining dependency without payment, waiting for money to return.
  • Flock's own marketing: "although some technologies require an upfront investment, they often result in significant savings over time." This is the lock-in pitch.

The "free" camera is the dealer's first hit.

Year one costs nothing. Year two costs $39,000. Year ten costs $390,000. By then, the department can't function without it and the council won't vote to remove it.

03 — The Cut

How To Kill It Before It Starts

  • Federal grants have public comment periods. Most residents never know they exist. SHSP and Byrne JAG grants require local government to publish notices — find them.
  • FOIA the grant application before it's approved. Request all pending federal grant applications from your police department. The application reveals the surveillance plan before cameras go up.
  • Monitor city council agendas. Grant acceptance resolutions must be voted on. This is your intervention window — often buried on the consent agenda where it passes without discussion.
  • Pull it off consent. The single most powerful intervention: show up to the council meeting where grant acceptance is on the consent agenda and request it be pulled for individual discussion. One resident. One request. Full public debate.
  • Use the BirdStrike templates. Public comment scripts, FOIA request language, and demand letter templates are ready to deploy. Go to BirdStrike →
  • If cameras are already up: FOIA the grant documentation. Undisclosed federal funding has its own accountability requirements — reporting obligations, use restrictions, audit trails — that Flock deployments frequently don't meet.

The grant pipeline is Flock's primary go-to-market strategy. It is also its most vulnerable point. Every dollar of federal money comes with federal strings. Pull them.

Check whether your city has pending federal grant applications for surveillance technology. If the cameras aren't up yet, you can stop them. If they are, the grant terms are your leverage.
